Career Choice

Why You Should Become a Programmer

Job Stability and Profitability
Programmers enjoy one of the fastest growing and best paying fields in the world. There is such a need for programmers that companies semi-frequently get into "bidding wars" over one employee.
You Typically Don't Have To Directly Deal with the Customer
If you've ever worked in retail, you know pretty well how stupid some customers can be. In programming, you usually won't have to deal with the customer directly and can instead let some other dedicated department deal with them instead. Of course, you won't be able to avoid them entirely, you will still need to help fix problems they may have.
Freedom (Relative to Other Careers)
Because of the extreme need for programmers, people often have a lot of power in deciding what is included in their contract, often times, this can include getting to choose what times you work, where you work, and how often you work. Though, you do still need to pull your weight, otherwise you will probably not have this freedom for a very long time.

Why I Will Enjoy Programming

I Already Do It For Fun
I don't know if you can tell, but I actually do this for fun, just have a look at my GitHub linked on the about page. I haven' been super active with public projects, mainly since these contain information about myself, and others close to me. I have over 5 active projects and 11 that sit in the background waiting to be picked up again.
It Isn't Retail
Retail sucks, if you need money, you tolerate it, but no one enjoys working as a low-level retail worker. They may tolerate it if they have friends there, and they may absolutely despise it. I am closer to the middle of the scale. If I could avoid dealing with the astounding number of Karens that I deal with every time I go to work, I would, in a heartbeat
For Real Though, It Isn't Retail
Retail is awful, anything that isn't retail is significantly better than retail.

Self Evaluation

I am relatively good at programming already, this isn't to say I am good, or anywhere close to that yet, I can figure out how to do most things, inefficiently and I lack many of the skills needed to write good code. If I need to know something at this point, I am able to look at the documentation for whatever I am using and find what it is I wanted to know. If I can't figure it out using the documentation, I can Google what I am trying to do, and usually find an answer. If neither of those work, I can typically still figure out a way to do whatever it is that I'm trying to do.

Given the fact that I already write code, almost daily, I think I am a perfect fit for the career. I would have to be insane to not apply my hobby to my career. This doesn't necessarily mean that I would be good at/enjoy writing code when other people are telling me to do their projects. I don't think that would be the case for me. Even if I started to dislike coding as a hobby, I have so many other extremely nerdy hobbies that the loss of one nerdy hobby isn't really a big deal. It may still cause a loss in up-to-dateness which wouldn't be particularly good, however, I think I would naturally keep up anyway. A few of my other hobbies require me to write code, they're the biggest reason I know so many languages on at least a basic level. My biggest flaw are perhaps my attempts to learn so many languages to their entirety without first taking a moment to stop and gain a whole bunch of experience with one thing.
